Why I built GaloDev in 30 days—and what almost killed it.

The first version had four tools, zero traffic, and one obvious problem. Three weeks in I almost shelved it. Then I changed one assumption about who I was building for, and everything broke open. Here's the rewrite, the numbers, and the parts I'd skip if I were starting again today.

The dumb version.

The first GaloDev had four tools: a password generator, a JSON formatter, a hex-to-RGB converter, and a "random number" page so embarrassing I deleted it six hours after pushing it live. The whole site looked like every other tool site on the internet — boxed inputs, blue buttons, three banner ads stacked above the tool. I was building another one. I just didn't know it yet.

I shipped it on a Tuesday. By Friday I had 72 unique visitors, mostly me on different browsers. By the following Tuesday I had 113. Nobody was sharing. Nobody was returning. The bounce rate was 91%. The dashboard looked like a flatline.

I had built the boring assumption: people search for "free password generator", land on whichever ranks first, copy the output, leave. That assumption is correct, and that assumption is exactly why the site was going to die. If you're a commodity, you compete on SEO and ad spend. I had neither.

Hitting the wall.

The morning of day 22 I almost killed the project. I had spent the previous Sunday writing a 4,000-word landing page about "privacy-first tools" and nobody had read past the fold. The analytics literally showed zero scroll. I sat at the desk for an hour wondering if this was the part where you quit, and decided, no, just one more reset.

I closed every tab and asked a question I should have asked on day one: "Why would someone come back to a tool site twice?" The honest answer was they wouldn't, unless coming back felt better than what they used yesterday. So the project pivoted, not in what it did, but in how it felt to use it.

Stop building "another free tool site". Start building the one that makes people screenshot a converter.

The rewrite that worked.

Three things changed in week four. None of them were about new tools. All of them were about the existing four tools.

1. A real design system, not a Tailwind preset

I committed to a typography pairing nobody else in the category uses — a sharp serif italic next to a clean grotesque — and built every component from a strict tokens file. Every tool inherited the same surface, the same glow button, the same monospace metadata. The brand became the consistency itself.

2. Tools that respect the visitor's eight seconds

I rewrote every tool to be operable in under three seconds. No splash screen, no "click to begin", no modal. The password generator generates a password on page load. The JSON formatter formats your paste the instant it lands. The hex converter accepts any color string and figures out the rest. The result is the page arrives already useful.

3. One ad. Always one. Never autoplay.

The original had three stacked ads above the tool. It looked like every other site. I went down to one — labeled, never videos, never interstitials. Revenue per visitor went up, not down, because the bounce rate collapsed from 91% to 38%.

Numbers, week by week.

What I won't pretend is that the rewrite produced a hockey stick. It produced a slope. Steady, week-on-week, the kind of growth that only matters in hindsight. Here's the actual progression for the first 90 days post-relaunch.

  • Week 5: 412 unique visitors. First Reddit comment that wasn't a bot.
  • Week 6: 1,148. A small dev newsletter linked the JSON formatter.
  • Week 8: 4,330. The password generator hit page 2 of Google for a long-tail keyword.
  • Week 12: 19,802. First return-visitor cohort visible in the dashboard.
  • Today: ~2.1M monthly. 48 tools. Zero employees.

Five things I'd do again.

  1. Ship before designing the system. The system emerged from the constraints of five working tools. If I'd designed it first, I'd have over-engineered.
  2. Pay for a real font. Instrument Serif is free, but the principle stands: typography is the cheapest, highest-leverage decision on the page.
  3. Write the manifesto early. The "no signup, no tracking, one ad" stance saved a hundred small product decisions.
  4. Build in public. The blog drives almost as much traffic as the tools themselves, and it cost me nothing to write.
  5. Refuse the freemium temptation. Three different "monetization advisors" told me to add a Pro tier. I'd be a third the size if I'd listened.

Three I wouldn't.

  1. Twitter threads. Spent six hours writing one, got 200 impressions. Never again.
  2. "Hunt of the Day" launches. Two hours of engagement, zero residual users. The audience there is wrong for tool sites.
  3. Building a "dashboard". Spent a weekend on user accounts before realizing nobody asked for them. git reset --hard erased every line.

Where we are now.

Eight months after the rewrite, GaloDev runs 48 tools, has 2.1M monthly visitors, pays me a respectable salary in dollars while I live in pesos, and most importantly — I still want to work on it. That last one is the metric you don't see in the analytics dashboard. It's the one that decides whether anything you build survives past month six.

If you're at week three and the dashboard is flat, the answer is almost never "ship more". It's almost always "look at what you have, and make it feel like nothing else."

— Galo
